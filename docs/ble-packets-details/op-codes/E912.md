# E912 Packets - Base + Chase / Circle-Chase Color (Not Yet Implemented)

**No rule currently exists for this opcode in the rules engine.** This doc captures the
working theory from lab observation, for use when building a rule.

Part of the same family as [E9 10](./e910.md), [E9 11](./e911.md), [E9 13](./e913.md), and
[E9 14](./e914.md) — see [E9 11](./e911.md#e911-packets---base--chase--paired-alternation-color-not-yet-implemented)
for the cross-opcode structural alignment. E9 12 carries 4 zone-color bytes (one more than E9
11's 3, one fewer than E9 13's 5).

Byte numbers left blank — no shipped rule exists yet. Note the `0x30` byte immediately after
the 4 zone-color bytes in both samples above, distinct from the `0F` marker seen elsewhere in
this family at the *start* of the color-byte block — this may be a second marker or sub-mode
selector specific to E9 12's longer payload structure, not yet investigated.

## Observed Behavior

**This is the best-supported opcode in the E9 10-E9 14 family for a specific, consistent
visual pattern:** the large majority of collected E9 12 samples (7+) describe a **circle/chase
animation** — lights stepping through positions UL → BL → BR → UR → Center in sequence, each
position holding for roughly 0.5-1 second before advancing — rather than the fade/cross-fade
behavior more typical of E9 11/E9 13. This is a stronger, more consistent signal than most of
this family currently has.

A few samples describe a fade-up/fade-down pattern instead (one color, all 5 LEDs, no
positional chase) — possibly a distinct sub-mode selected by a byte not yet identified, or a
different opcode variant entirely (one such sample has a notably shorter payload than the
circle-chase samples — worth checking whether it's actually a different, shorter sub-format
under the same opcode rather than a variant of the main format).

## Timing

Same standard timing-byte formula as the rest of this family (see [E9 11](./e911.md#timing)) —
not independently confirmed for E9 12, working assumption only. The observed "lights stay in
one spot for ~0.5-1s then change" cadence across circle-chase samples gives a rough per-step
timing reference, but hasn't been formally correlated against the timing byte's bit fields the
way E9 13's sweep was.

## Building Your Own

The circle-chase pattern is your best starting point for a first rule attempt in this family,
given its consistency across samples. Copy a working sample's trailer verbatim and vary the 4
zone-color bytes and timing byte; treat the animation *speed*/step-timing as unconfirmed
rather than assuming it's controllable via the timing byte alone.

## Suggested Lab Tests

1. **`0x30` byte investigation** — determine whether this recurring byte immediately after the
   zone-color block is a required constant, a sub-mode selector, or coincidental across the
   small sample set.
2. **Circle-chase step-timing isolation** — hold zone colors and the `0x30`-position byte
   constant, sweep the timing byte, and measure per-step dwell time (not just total on-time)
   to determine whether the timing byte controls chase speed directly.
3. **Short-payload variant investigation** — the fade-up/fade-down sample with a shorter
   payload needs more examples to determine if it's a real sub-mode or an isolated anomaly.
4. **Zone-byte role isolation** — same one-byte-at-a-time override methodology used on E9 13,
   to confirm whether the 4 zone bytes represent 4 independent chase positions (consistent
   with the observed 5-position circle including Center, if Center is driven by a 5th implicit
   value) or some other structure.
