### Bytes 2-3 - No Observable Effect (confirmed across E9 05/09/0E)

Purpose unknown. Can be omitted.
Other values: `0xE100`

**Resolved: `E100` vs. `E200` makes no observable difference, tested across three opcodes.**
This was originally raised as a possible explanation for the `t=4`/`t=6` timing anomaly (the
idea being a stray restart could throw off an on-time/flash-count reading). It was ruled out
as the explanation for _that specific_ anomaly early on вАФ the original anomalous readings and
their clean retests used the identical `E200` prefix, so the prefix didn't change between the
"wrong" and "right" measurements there. The broader cross-opcode test (deferred at the time,
see the note that used to be here) has since been run: `E100` and `E200` were sent back-to-back
at matched timing bytes across E9 05, E9 09, and E9 0E, same observer, watching for any
difference at all вАФ timing, restart behavior, anything. No difference was observed on any of
the three. Bytes 2-3 can be treated as not mattering for behavior (though still required as a
well-formed prefix) across every opcode tested so far.

| --- | ---------------- | ----------------- | ---------------- | --------------- | ------------------ |
| 1   | 1.5s             | 1.5s              | 3                | 3               | Yes                |
| 2   | 3.0s             | 3.0s              | 6                | 6               | Yes                |
| 3   | 4.75s            | 4.5s              | 9                | 9               | Yes                |
| 4   | ~6s (retested)   | 6.0s              | 12 (retested)    | 12              | Yes                |
| 5   | 8.0s             | 7.5s              | (not recorded)   | 15              | Yes (on-time only) |
| 6   | 9s (retested)    | 9.0s              | ~18 (retested)   | 18              | Yes                |
| 7   | 11.25s           | 10.5s             | 21               | 21              | Yes                |

Every tested point (1-7) now fits cleanly, residuals of 0-0.75s throughout вАФ the same kind of
small stopwatch-precision error seen everywhere else in this project's timing tests. The
original `t=4`/`t=6` readings (8.0s/16 flashes and 4.0s/8 flashes respectively) don't match
either their own retest or each other's formula prediction in a way that suggests a clean
swap between the two вАФ most likely they were independent one-off mistiming/miscounting on the
first pass, not a real property of those two `t` values. **Treat the formula as reliable
across the full 1-7 range now.**

**Flash rate is scaler-dependent, and a third rate has now been found under extended-timeout
mode.** Three distinct rates confirmed so far:

| Mode                                 | On-time formula                                                                                           | Flash rate                                                                             |
| ------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Normal, scaler=0                     | `1.5 √Ч t`                                                                                                 | 2 flashes/sec                                                                          |
| Normal, scaler=1                     | `3.0 √Ч t` (matches E9 05/09 exactly)                                                                      | 1 flash/sec                                                                            |
| Extended-timeout (bit 7=1), scaler=0 | `7.6 √Ч t` (matches E9 05/09's extended formula almost exactly вАФ `7.6√Ч2=15.2s` predicted vs. 15s observed) | ~0.3-0.4 flashes/sec (4-6 flashes over 15s вАФ roughly one flash every 2.5-3.75 seconds) |

This is now the **third** independently-confirmed case of an E9 05/09 timing formula
transferring cleanly to E9 0E (base scaler=0, base scaler=1, and now extended-timeout) вАФ good
evidence the underlying timing engine really is shared across opcodes, with E9 0E layering its
own flash-rate behavior on top rather than having a fundamentally different timing system.
**Extended-timeout mode's much slower flash rate makes it a good choice if you need to
manually count flashes for future testing** вАФ a lot easier to count 4-6 flashes over 15
seconds by eye than 6 flashes crammed into 3 seconds.

**Fade timing does not follow the E9 05/09 formula вАФ now confirmed reproducible, not a
one-off.** A repeat test of `t=2` with `fade_bits=01` (byte `0x12`), sent 3 additional times,
came back at **~0.25s fade every single time** вАФ consistently half the 0.5s the E9 05/09
formula predicts. This is no longer a suspected measurement fluke; it's a real, repeatable
deviation specific to this opcode. Combined with the earlier finding that `fade_bits=10` was
inconsistent elsewhere in this project (sometimes 0.5s, sometimes 1.0s), there's now solid
reason to treat E9 0E's fade timing as governed by its own rules, separate from the on-time
formula that _did_ transfer cleanly three times over.

**`fade_bits=11` is resolved: fixed ~1.5s fade with a "slow flash" character, on-time
formula unaffected.** A repeat/gap-fill test at `t=2`, `t=4`, and `t=8` (all `fade_bits=11`)
came back clean and consistent: on-time matched the standard `1.5√Чt` formula exactly at every
point (~3.5s, ~6-6.5s, ~12-12.5s respectively), each followed by the same ~1.5s fade-out
described as having a distinct "slow flash" quality rather than a smooth dim. This supersedes
the earlier single-reading impression (below) that this value produced a genuinely non-linear
fade curve or a separate slow on-time phase вАФ the clean 3-point repeat set found neither; it's
a fixed-duration fade with a different visual character than `fade_bits=01`'s smooth ~0.25s
fade, not a different curve shape or duration formula.

**The byte-15 fade-curve difference noted in an earlier repeatability test does not hold up
under a direct side-by-side comparison.** `0x50` was previously flagged as possibly fading
differently than baseline (`0x19`) based on notes from a repeatability test. A dedicated
side-by-side comparison вАФ both at the same timing byte, one right after the other вАФ described
**both** as a "linear fading curve," with no distinguishable difference noted. Best explained
as a subjective impression from the earlier test rather than a real effect; not worth treating
as a confirmed finding going forward.

**Flash count is confirmed perfectly reproducible at low `t` values, but genuinely random at
byte15=`0x0A`.** Three repeats each of `t=1` and `t=2` (at baseline byte 15) produced identical
flash counts every time (3 and 6 respectively, no variance at all) вАФ ruling out the idea that
flash-count randomness is a general property of low `t`. In contrast, five separate sends of
the "5-7 sec" packet (`byte15=0x0A`) produced flash counts of 7, 4, 5, 4, and 6 вАФ genuinely
different each time, confirming the randomness is a real, isolated property of that specific
byte-15 value rather than something that shows up everywhere.

**Flash pattern is confirmed to vary between individual flashes within a single "on" period,
not just between separate sends.** Multiple notes across this testing describe "different
flash pattern each pass" even within one packet's single continuous on-time вАФ this is a
distinct thing from the earlier byte-15 "seed" finding (which was about _which_ byte-15 value
produces stable vs. chaotic behavior); this is about the pattern visibly varying flash-to-flash
even under the _stable_ byte-15 value. Consistent with a genuinely randomized element built
into the strobe itself, not just a per-packet seed.

### Byte 14 - Strobe Enable (working theory)

**Confirmed to matter, but not yet understood as a real parameter.** A dedicated
one-byte-at-a-time isolation sweep tested `0x00`, `0x30`, `0x90`, and `0xff` against the known
baseline `0x59` вАФ every single non-baseline value produced **solid-on, no strobing at all**
(all 5 LEDs lit continuously for the full 3s, no flashing), while `0x59` alone gives the
confirmed 6-flash strobe. That's a clean, total behavior change across a wide value range with
no partial/graduated effect вАФ consistent with this being either a strobe-enable flag that
requires one specific value (not a bit pattern), or a checksum-style field where any mismatch
makes the device fall back to a safe default (solid-on) rather than reject the packet outright
(contrast with byte 18 below, which causes outright non-response instead). Not yet enough data
to tell those two theories apart вАФ see follow-ups.

### Byte 15 - Flash Pattern / Seed (confirmed: selects a pattern _shape_, not just sync/chaos)

**The strongest evidence for the "seed" hypothesis in this whole opcode.** A sweep across
`0x00, 0x0A, 0x19(baseline), 0x30, 0x50, 0xFF` produced genuinely different _qualities_ of
behavior, not a linear progression:

| Byte 15                                   | Observed behavior                                                                      |
| ----------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `0x00`                                    | Barely functional вАФ only 1-2 LEDs flash, ~2 times, very slow, inconsistent which LED   |
| `0x0A`                                    | Chaotic вАФ flash count varies 2-5 per send, not all LEDs flash the same number of times |
| `0x19` (baseline)                         | Clean, synchronized вАФ 6 flashes, all LEDs together, repeatable                         |
| `0x30`                                    | Same flash _count_ as baseline (6) but the pattern itself is random each time it fires |
| `0x50`                                    | Also clean and synchronized вАФ 6 flashes, all LEDs together (same _category_ of         |
| result as baseline, different byte value) |
| `0xFF`                                    | Chaotic вАФ only 2 flashes, inconsistent which LEDs, no repeatable pattern               |

**Follow-up isolation sweep confirmed more values land in the chaotic category, and
surfaced a third, distinct category.** With byte 16 held fixed at the confirmed baseline
(`0x02`) so results are attributable to byte 15 alone, `0x07`, `0x0A`, `0x0F`, and `0x17` all
landed cleanly in the chaotic/not-synced-stable category, consistent with `0x0A`/`0xFF` above.
`0x32` stood apart from both categories: described as "chaotic, but a pattern does emerge (all
seem to pulse within ~200ms of each other, but not in the same order)" вАФ looser than clean
sync, tighter than pure chaos.

A later, more detailed look at `0x32` (t=4, byte 16=0x02) sharpened this further: rather than
just "loose sync," the flashes follow a **directional, edge-in spawn order** вАФ e.g. the top of
the wand flashes in/close to sync with the center, the second-from-top segment close to the
bottom, the third-from-top on its own вАФ described as having some spawn variance but a real,
repeatable directional shape, not randomness. Compared side-by-side in the same session,
`0x19` (baseline) presented as async but tightly bounded (all LEDs flashing within ~0.5s of
each other, no strong direction), while `0x50` presented as fully in sync вАФ so even within the
values that don't fall into the "chaotic" bucket, byte 15 distinguishes at least three
qualitatively different pattern shapes: tight sync (`0x50`), loose/async-but-bounded (`0x19`),
and directional edge-in (`0x32`). **Revised theory: byte 15 is a seed that selects a pattern
_shape_ from a small family of qualitatively different outcomes (at least: tight sync, loose
async, directional edge-in, and several flavors of chaos), not a simple binary
stable-vs-chaotic switch.** Given this, byte 15 is better modeled as an enum of a few known
pattern shapes than as a continuous or formulaic parameter вАФ there's no evidence of an
underlying numeric relationship between the byte value and which shape it produces.

**Repeatability test done вАФ `0x50` retested 3 times, then directly compared against
baseline.** All 3 reps gave the same _category_ of result (3s on, 6 flashes, synced),
consistent with `0x50` reliably landing on stable/synced output. An earlier pass at this test
surfaced a possible fade-curve difference between `0x50` and baseline, but **a dedicated
side-by-side comparison ruled this out** вАФ both `0x50` and baseline (`0x19`) were
independently described as a "linear fading curve" with no distinguishable difference. The
original observation is best explained as a subjective impression rather than a real effect;
byte 15 does not appear to meaningfully affect fade character, only pattern shape/stability.

**Flash count is confirmed perfectly reproducible at "clean" byte-15 values, genuinely random
at unstable ones.** Three repeats each of `t=1` and `t=2` (both at baseline byte 15, `0x19`)
gave identical flash counts every time вАФ no variance at all. In contrast, five sends of the
"5-7 sec" packet (`byte15=0x0A`) gave flash counts of 7, 4, 5, 4, and 6 вАФ genuinely different
each time. This confirms the randomness lives specifically in certain byte-15 values (like
`0x0A`, `0xFF`), not as a general property of the opcode or of low `t` вАФ reinforces the "seed"
theory, since a real seed would be expected to land some values on stable output and others on
chaotic output, exactly this pattern.

Separately, multiple tests noted **flash pattern varying between individual flashes within a
single packet's on-time**, not just between separate sends of the same packet вАФ e.g. a note
of "different flash pattern each pass" describing one continuous 3-second strobe. This is a
distinct phenomenon from the seed-vs-chaotic split above; it suggests there's a genuinely
randomized element to which specific LEDs flash on each individual pulse, layered on top of
whatever byte 15 controls about overall pattern shape.

**Practical takeaway for rule construction:** treat byte 15 as picking between a small set of
known pattern shapes (e.g. `0x19`/`0x50` for a stable look, `0x32` for a directional edge-in
look) rather than trying to map it to a continuous "spawn rate" вАФ the data doesn't support a
formula there, and the isolated sweep above (byte 16 held fixed) rules out most of the range
as simply chaotic. See Byte 16 below for why that byte doesn't look like a clean rate control
either.

### Byte 16 - Flash Count, bit 5 = mode switch (confirmed); remaining bits likely non-deterministic

**Confirmed clean and simple for bit 5**, unlike byte 15. Every tested value with bit 5
(`0x20`) clear gives 6 flashes; every value with bit 5 set gives 4 flashes вАФ a clean binary
split, not a graduated count:

| Byte 16                                   | Bit 5 | Flashes |
| ----------------------------------------- | ----- | ------- |
| `0x00`, `0x01`, `0x02` (baseline), `0x14` | 0     | 6       |
| `0x30`, `0xFF`                            | 1     | 4       |

This is a genuine, reliable, bit-level rule вАФ worth trusting for future packet construction.
**Other-bit sweep done** (`0x00, 0x04, 0x08, 0x10`, all bit-5-clear): every value produced the
identical 6-flash, 3s-on baseline behavior. Confirms bit 5 really is the only thing that
matters in this byte, at least across the range tested вАФ safe to treat the other 7 bits as
inert and leave them at any convenient value when constructing new packets, as long as bit 5
is set correctly for the desired flash-count family.

**A follow-up isolation sweep looking for a fade/spawn-rate relationship in the remaining bits
found evidence of non-determinism instead of a clean trend.** With byte 15 held fixed at the
confirmed baseline (`0x19`), byte 16 was swept across `0x00, 0x08, 0x20, 0x40, 0x80, 0xFF`
watching fade-out duration specifically. `0x08` produced ~0.25s fade, `0x20` produced ~0.5s
fade вАФ a tempting trend вАФ but **`0x80` was tested twice and produced both results** (~0.25s on
one send, ~0.5s on a repeat, nothing else changed). That's the same signature already
documented for `fade_bits=10` elsewhere in this opcode (confirmed inconsistent between 0.5s
and 1.0s) вАФ evidence this byte, outside its confirmed bit-5 flash-count switch, does not behave
as a clean deterministic control. **Do not treat byte 16 (beyond bit 5) as a reliable rate
parameter** вАФ the `0x08`/`0x20` single readings that looked like a trend are equally
explainable as the same underlying randomness landing differently, given `0x80` demonstrably
does exactly that. A repeat test at `0x08` and `0x20` would confirm whether they're also
inconsistent, but given `0x80`'s result, that's the working assumption until shown otherwise.

### Byte 17 - Same pattern as Byte 14 (working theory)

Tested `0x00`, `0x20`, `0x80`, `0xff` against baseline `0x48` вАФ identical result to byte 14's
sweep: every non-baseline value produced solid-on, no strobing. Given both bytes 14 and 17
show the exact same all-or-nothing behavior pattern, they may be two halves of one larger
validation field (e.g. a 16-bit checksum split across two byte positions) rather than two
independent single-byte parameters.

**Combined test done** вАФ both bytes changed together (not just separately), tested at three
value pairs (`00/00`, `ff/ff`, and non-matching `30/20`). All three produced the same solid-on
fallback as changing either byte alone вАФ no new or different failure mode appeared when both
were wrong simultaneously. This doesn't distinguish between "two independent checks" and "two
halves of one combined field" (both theories would produce this same result), but it does rule
out a third, more complex failure mode that only appears when both are invalid at once.

### Byte 18 - Fixed Constant, Not a Computed Checksum (revised theory)

**The strongest evidence of a checksum in the whole packet вАФ and now, evidence against it
being a _computed_ one.** Every non-baseline value tested (`0x00`, `0x50`, `0xD0`, `0xFF`)
resulted in **complete non-response** вАФ the device ignored the packet outright, tested 3 times
per value with no reaction at all. This is categorically different from bytes 14/17 (which
still produced _some_ behavior, just not strobing) вАФ total silence is strong evidence this
byte is validated before the device will act on the packet at all.

**Cross-payload test confirms it: `0xAE` is required regardless of payload, not computed per
packet.** The same byte-18 sweep (`0x00`, `0x50`, `0xD0`, `0xFF`) was repeated on a
second, substantially different base packet вАФ different colors (4 distinct values instead of
one repeated color) and a different timing byte вАФ with byte 19 held fixed this time (an
earlier attempt at this test had accidentally varied byte 19 instead of byte 18). **All 4
non-`0xAE` values produced the identical result: complete non-response, confirmed watched and
observed, not just an unlogged batch.** If this byte were a real computed checksum, changing
the colors and timing byte should very plausibly have changed what value byte 18 needed to be
for a response вАФ it didn't. `0xAE` was required on both wildly different payloads. **This is
solid evidence byte 18 is a fixed constant this opcode expects, not a per-packet computed
value** вАФ likely a fixed protocol/format marker for E9 0E specifically, in the same spirit as
bytes 14 and 17, rather than a validation checksum in the traditional sense. The
"checksum-vs-vibration-byte" alternative theory is also resolved: a separate test confirmed
byte 19 can be changed freely with zero effect on timing, so byte 18 and byte 19 are
independent, and byte 18 is not simply a second vibration parameter.

**Practical takeaway: always use `0xAE` for byte 18 in this opcode**, regardless of what
colors or timing you're using вАФ no computation needed.

### Byte 19 - Vibration Pattern

See [EMCOT.world](https://emcot.world/Disney_MagicBand%2B_Bluetooth_Codes#Vibration_Palette) for more information.

**Confirmed independent of strobe timing.** A test varying this byte between `0xB0` and `0x00`
(holding everything else, including byte 18, fixed) produced identical ~25s on-time in both

interact with the strobe's visual timing.

## Building Your Own

```text
83 01 <E1|E2> 00 E9 0E 00 <TIMING> 0F <MASK+COLOR> <MASK+COLOR> <MASK+COLOR> <MASK+COLOR> <MASK+COLOR> 59 <BYTE15> <BYTE16> 48 AE <VIBRATION>
```

**Formula confirmed clean across the full tested range.** There is now a reliable formula for
picking a `<TIMING>` byte, with three flash-rate variants depending on mode:

```
Normal, scaler=0:             on_time = 1.5 √Ч t    flashes = 3 √Ч t         (2 flashes/sec)
Normal, scaler=1:             on_time = 3.0 √Ч t    flashes = 1 √Ч t         (1 flash/sec)
Extended-timeout, scaler=0:   on_time = 7.6 √Ч t    flash rate ~0.3-0.4/sec (much slower, easier to count)
```

`byte = (extended << 7) | (scaler << 6) | (fade_bits << 4) | t`. **Fade timing does not follow
the E9 05/09 formula for this opcode вАФ confirmed, not just suspected.** `fade_bits=01`
consistently measures ~0.25s (confirmed across 4 total sends), about half the 0.5s the E9
05/09 formula would predict. **`fade_bits=11` is now fully resolved: on-time still follows the
normal `1.5√Чt` formula unchanged вАФ there is no separate slow phase or doubled duration вАФ but
the fade-out itself is a fixed ~1.5s with a distinct "slow flash" character**, rather than a
smooth dim. Confirmed clean and consistent across `t=2` (~3.5s on, matches `1.5√Ч2=3.0s`),
`t=4` (~6-6.5s on, matches `1.5√Ч4=6.0s`), and `t=8` (~12-12.5s on, matches `1.5√Ч8=12.0s`), all
followed by the same ~1.5s slow-flash fade. Earlier language describing this as a
"non-linear fade" or a possible second on-time phase came from an initial, inconsistent
small sample (single readings at `t=1`, `t=4`, `t=6` that didn't extrapolate cleanly against
each other) вАФ the clean repeat set above supersedes that. Don't assume fade timing or shape
transfers from E9 05/09 for this opcode; test the specific `fade_bits` value you plan to use.

**`t=4` and `t=6`, previously flagged as exceptions, have been retested and now fit the
formula cleanly** вАФ the original odd readings were most likely one-off measurement errors, not
a real property of those values. The formula can now be trusted across the full 1-7 range.

**Worked example вАФ a ~10 second strobe:** `t вЙИ 10/1.5 вЙИ 7` (matches an actual tested value:
7 вЖТ 11.25s observed, 21 flashes) вЖТ `byte = (0<<7)|(0<<6)|(0b00<<4)|0b0111 = 0x07`.

**Worked example вАФ a slow strobe for manual flash-counting** (e.g. while testing something
else and needing to reliably count flashes by eye): use extended-timeout mode at a small `t`,
e.g. `t=2` вЖТ `byte = (1<<7)|(0<<6)|(0b00<<4)|0b0010 = 0x82`, confirmed at 15s on, 4-6 flashes,
~1s fade вАФ roughly one flash every 2.5-3.75 seconds, much easier to count than the base 2/sec
rate.

### `<MASK+COLOR>` (byte per LED zone)

Same model as E9 09: `mask_color = (mask << 5) | color_index`, mask=`101` in every packet
seen so far. Zone order confirmed as Center, Top-Right, Bottom-Right, Bottom-Left, Top-Left.

## Suggested follow-up tests

1. **Confirm byte 16's non-determinism with repeats at `0x08` and `0x20`.** `0x80` was shown to
   produce two different fade durations on repeat sends with nothing else changed вАФ worth
   confirming `0x08`/`0x20` show the same inconsistency (rather than being genuine, reliable
   trend points) before fully closing this out. Given `0x80`'s result, expect inconsistency, but
   this hasn't been directly confirmed for those two specific values yet.
2. **Map the boundary between byte 15's known pattern-shape values, if a finer level of
   control is ever needed.** Current confirmed shapes: `0x50` (tight sync), `0x19` (loose/
   async-but-bounded), `0x32` (directional edge-in), plus a broad chaotic region covering most
   of the rest of the byte's range. This is enough to treat byte 15 as a small enum for
   practical rule-building; further mapping is a nice-to-have, not a gap blocking use.
3. **No formulaic spawn-rate or fade-rate control has been found for this opcode** вАФ neither
   byte 15 nor byte 16 (outside its confirmed bit-5 switch) behaves like a continuous rate
   parameter suitable for driving a WLED effect's speed/intensity sliders directly. For
   practical use (e.g. approximating this look with a WLED effect like Colortwinkles), the
   recommended approach is a fixed speed/intensity chosen by eye, with byte 15's confirmed
   pattern-shape values optionally mapped to a small set of alternate looks/presets rather than
   to a continuous slider value.
