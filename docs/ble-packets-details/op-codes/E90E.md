timing behaves fundamentally differently from E9 05/E9 09 тАФ the confirmed timing-byte formula
from those opcodes does **not** carry over here. Treat this as its own model, not an extension

## Byte Details

### Bytes 0-1 - Disney Identifier

Always the Disney identifier. Required.

### Bytes 2-3 - Unknown

Purpose unknown. Can be omitted.
Other values: `0xE100`

**One hypothesis worth recording: does `E100` restart an in-progress effect while `E200`
lets it continue?** This was raised as a possible explanation for the `t=4`/`t=6` timing
anomaly (the idea being a stray restart could throw off an on-time/flash-count reading). It's
ruled out as the explanation for _that specific_ anomaly тАФ checking the packet table, both the
original anomalous readings and their clean retests used the identical `E200` prefix, so the
prefix didn't change between the "wrong" and "right" measurements. The retests turning out
clean is better explained by ordinary mistiming/miscounting on the first pass.

**Deliberately deferred rather than tested here.** If bytes 2-3 have a real, consistent
meaning, it's most likely opcode-agnostic тАФ this field sits outside the opcode itself
(`E9 0E`), so whatever it does probably applies the same way to E9 05, E9 09, and every other
opcode too. Testing it in isolation on just this one opcode risks either wasted effort (if it
turns out to need cross-opcode context to interpret properly) or a false conclusion (mistaking
an E9 0E-specific quirk for a general rule). The plan is to hold off until enough other
opcodes are characterized to test this once, across several already-understood opcodes at
once тАФ that'll be both more conclusive and less repetitive than testing it separately for
each opcode as it comes up.

| 3   | 4.75s            | 4.5s              | 9                | 9               | Yes                |
| 4   | ~6s (retested)   | 6.0s              | 12 (retested)    | 12              | Yes                |
| 5   | 8.0s             | 7.5s              | (not recorded)   | 15              | Yes (on-time only) |
| 6   | 9s (retested)    | 9.0s              | ~18 (retested)   | 18              | Yes                |
| 7   | 11.25s           | 10.5s             | 21               | 21              | Yes                |

Every tested point (1-7) now fits cleanly, residuals of 0-0.75s throughout тАФ the same kind of
small stopwatch-precision error seen everywhere else in this project's timing tests. The
original `t=4`/`t=6` readings (8.0s/16 flashes and 4.0s/8 flashes respectively) don't match
either their own retest or each other's formula prediction in a way that suggests a clean
swap between the two тАФ most likely they were independent one-off mistiming/miscounting on the
first pass, not a real property of those two `t` values. **Treat the formula as reliable
across the full 1-7 range now.**

**Flash rate is scaler-dependent, and a third rate has now been found under extended-timeout
mode.** Three distinct rates confirmed so far:

| Mode                                 | On-time formula                                                                                           | Flash rate                                                                             |
| ------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Normal, scaler=0                     | `1.5 ├Ч t`                                                                                                 | 2 flashes/sec                                                                          |
| Normal, scaler=1                     | `3.0 ├Ч t` (matches E9 05/09 exactly)                                                                      | 1 flash/sec                                                                            |
| Extended-timeout (bit 7=1), scaler=0 | `7.6 ├Ч t` (matches E9 05/09's extended formula almost exactly тАФ `7.6├Ч2=15.2s` predicted vs. 15s observed) | ~0.3-0.4 flashes/sec (4-6 flashes over 15s тАФ roughly one flash every 2.5-3.75 seconds) |

This is now the **third** independently-confirmed case of an E9 05/09 timing formula
transferring cleanly to E9 0E (base scaler=0, base scaler=1, and now extended-timeout) тАФ good
evidence the underlying timing engine really is shared across opcodes, with E9 0E layering its
own flash-rate behavior on top rather than having a fundamentally different timing system.
**Extended-timeout mode's much slower flash rate makes it a good choice if you need to
manually count flashes for future testing** тАФ a lot easier to count 4-6 flashes over 15
seconds by eye than 6 flashes crammed into 3 seconds.

**Fade timing does not follow the E9 05/09 formula тАФ now confirmed reproducible, not a
one-off.** A repeat test of `t=2` with `fade_bits=01` (byte `0x12`), sent 3 additional times,
came back at **~0.25s fade every single time** тАФ consistently half the 0.5s the E9 05/09
formula predicts. This is no longer a suspected measurement fluke; it's a real, repeatable
deviation specific to this opcode. Combined with the earlier finding that `fade_bits=10` was
inconsistent elsewhere in this project (sometimes 0.5s, sometimes 1.0s), there's now solid
reason to treat E9 0E's fade timing as governed by its own rules, separate from the on-time
formula that _did_ transfer cleanly three times over.

**`fade_bits=11` (byte `0x32`) revealed something new: a non-linear fade curve.** Rather than
a simple duration, this value was described as fading out with the rate itself changing
partway through ("as it progresses fade out slows down") тАФ qualitatively different from every
other fade tested in this opcode so far (`fade_bits=01` at both byte-15 baseline `0x19` and
`0x50` were both independently described as a "linear fading curve"). This suggests
`fade_bits` might not just scale a single fade duration for this opcode тАФ different values may
select genuinely different fade _shapes_ (linear vs. decelerating), not just different
lengths of the same shape. Worth deliberate testing to characterize `fade_bits=11`'s curve
more precisely (e.g. noting brightness at several points through the fade, not just total
duration) before assuming it's just "slow."

**The byte-15 fade-curve difference noted in an earlier repeatability test does not hold up
under a direct side-by-side comparison.** `0x50` was previously flagged as possibly fading
differently than baseline (`0x19`) based on notes from a repeatability test. A dedicated
side-by-side comparison тАФ both at the same timing byte, one right after the other тАФ described
**both** as a "linear fading curve," with no distinguishable difference noted. Best explained
as a subjective impression from the earlier test rather than a real effect; not worth treating
as a confirmed finding going forward.

**Flash count is confirmed perfectly reproducible at low `t` values, but genuinely random at
byte15=`0x0A`.** Three repeats each of `t=1` and `t=2` (at baseline byte 15) produced identical
flash counts every time (3 and 6 respectively, no variance at all) тАФ ruling out the idea that
flash-count randomness is a general property of low `t`. In contrast, five separate sends of
the "5-7 sec" packet (`byte15=0x0A`) produced flash counts of 7, 4, 5, 4, and 6 тАФ genuinely
different each time, confirming the randomness is a real, isolated property of that specific
byte-15 value rather than something that shows up everywhere.

**Flash pattern is confirmed to vary between individual flashes within a single "on" period,
not just between separate sends.** Multiple notes across this testing describe "different
flash pattern each pass" even within one packet's single continuous on-time тАФ this is a
distinct thing from the earlier byte-15 "seed" finding (which was about _which_ byte-15 value
produces stable vs. chaotic behavior); this is about the pattern visibly varying flash-to-flash
even under the _stable_ byte-15 value. Consistent with a genuinely randomized element built
into the strobe itself, not just a per-packet seed.

### Byte 14 - Strobe Enable (working theory)

**Confirmed to matter, but not yet understood as a real parameter.** A dedicated
one-byte-at-a-time isolation sweep tested `0x00`, `0x30`, `0x90`, and `0xff` against the known
baseline `0x59` тАФ every single non-baseline value produced **solid-on, no strobing at all**
(all 5 LEDs lit continuously for the full 3s, no flashing), while `0x59` alone gives the
confirmed 6-flash strobe. That's a clean, total behavior change across a wide value range with
no partial/graduated effect тАФ consistent with this being either a strobe-enable flag that
requires one specific value (not a bit pattern), or a checksum-style field where any mismatch
makes the device fall back to a safe default (solid-on) rather than reject the packet outright
(contrast with byte 18 below, which causes outright non-response instead). Not yet enough data
to tell those two theories apart тАФ see follow-ups.

### Byte 15 - Flash Pattern / Seed (working theory)

**The strongest evidence for the "seed" hypothesis in this whole opcode.** A sweep across
`0x00, 0x0A, 0x19(baseline), 0x30, 0x50, 0xFF` produced genuinely different _qualities_ of
behavior, not a linear progression:

| Byte 15                                   | Observed behavior                                                                      |
| ----------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| `0x00`                                    | Barely functional тАФ only 1-2 LEDs flash, ~2 times, very slow, inconsistent which LED   |
| `0x0A`                                    | Chaotic тАФ flash count varies 2-5 per send, not all LEDs flash the same number of times |
| `0x19` (baseline)                         | Clean, synchronized тАФ 6 flashes, all LEDs together, repeatable                         |
| `0x30`                                    | Same flash _count_ as baseline (6) but the pattern itself is random each time it fires |
| `0x50`                                    | Also clean and synchronized тАФ 6 flashes, all LEDs together (same _category_ of         |
| result as baseline, different byte value) |
| `0xFF`                                    | Chaotic тАФ only 2 flashes, inconsistent which LEDs, no repeatable pattern               |

This is not a monotonic or simple-formula relationship тАФ it looks much more like a **seed
value feeding a pattern generator**, where some seed values land on stable, synchronized
output and others land on chaotic, non-repeating output, the same way a small change to a
random seed can produce a completely different-looking sequence. This directly supports the
"pattern/seed" hypothesis.

**Repeatability test done тАФ `0x50` retested 3 times, then directly compared against
baseline.** All 3 reps gave the same _category_ of result (3s on, 6 flashes, synced),
consistent with `0x50` reliably landing on stable/synced output. An earlier pass at this test
surfaced a possible fade-curve difference between `0x50` and baseline, but **a dedicated
side-by-side comparison ruled this out** тАФ both `0x50` and baseline (`0x19`) were
independently described as a "linear fading curve" with no distinguishable difference. The
original observation is best explained as a subjective impression rather than a real effect;
byte 15 does not appear to meaningfully affect fade character, only pattern stability/sync.

**Flash count is confirmed perfectly reproducible at "clean" byte-15 values, genuinely random
at unstable ones.** Three repeats each of `t=1` and `t=2` (both at baseline byte 15, `0x19`)
gave identical flash counts every time тАФ no variance at all. In contrast, five sends of the
"5-7 sec" packet (`byte15=0x0A`) gave flash counts of 7, 4, 5, 4, and 6 тАФ genuinely different
each time. This confirms the randomness lives specifically in certain byte-15 values (like
`0x0A`, `0xFF`), not as a general property of the opcode or of low `t` тАФ reinforces the "seed"
theory, since a real seed would be expected to land some values on stable output and others on
chaotic output, exactly this pattern.

Separately, multiple tests noted **flash pattern varying between individual flashes within a
single packet's on-time**, not just between separate sends of the same packet тАФ e.g. a note
of "different flash pattern each pass" describing one continuous 3-second strobe. This is a
distinct phenomenon from the seed-vs-chaotic split above; it suggests there's a genuinely
randomized element to which specific LEDs flash on each individual pulse, layered on top of
whatever byte 15 controls about overall stability.

### Byte 16 - Flash Count, bit 5 = mode switch (confirmed)

**Confirmed clean and simple**, unlike byte 15. Every tested value with bit 5 (`0x20`) clear
gives 6 flashes; every value with bit 5 set gives 4 flashes тАФ a clean binary split, not a
graduated count:

| Byte 16                                   | Bit 5 | Flashes |
| ----------------------------------------- | ----- | ------- |
| `0x00`, `0x01`, `0x02` (baseline), `0x14` | 0     | 6       |
| `0x30`, `0xFF`                            | 1     | 4       |

This is a genuine, reliable, bit-level rule тАФ worth trusting for future packet construction.
**Other-bit sweep done** (`0x00, 0x04, 0x08, 0x10`, all bit-5-clear): every value produced the
identical 6-flash, 3s-on baseline behavior. Confirms bit 5 really is the only thing that
matters in this byte, at least across the range tested тАФ safe to treat the other 7 bits as
inert and leave them at any convenient value when constructing new packets, as long as bit 5
is set correctly for the desired flash-count family.

### Byte 17 - Same pattern as Byte 14 (working theory)

Tested `0x00`, `0x20`, `0x80`, `0xff` against baseline `0x48` тАФ identical result to byte 14's
sweep: every non-baseline value produced solid-on, no strobing. Given both bytes 14 and 17
show the exact same all-or-nothing behavior pattern, they may be two halves of one larger
validation field (e.g. a 16-bit checksum split across two byte positions) rather than two
independent single-byte parameters.

**Combined test done** тАФ both bytes changed together (not just separately), tested at three
value pairs (`00/00`, `ff/ff`, and non-matching `30/20`). All three produced the same solid-on
fallback as changing either byte alone тАФ no new or different failure mode appeared when both
were wrong simultaneously. This doesn't distinguish between "two independent checks" and "two
halves of one combined field" (both theories would produce this same result), but it does rule
out a third, more complex failure mode that only appears when both are invalid at once.

### Byte 18 - Fixed Constant, Not a Computed Checksum (revised theory)

**The strongest evidence of a checksum in the whole packet тАФ and now, evidence against it
being a _computed_ one.** Every non-baseline value tested (`0x00`, `0x50`, `0xD0`, `0xFF`)
resulted in **complete non-response** тАФ the device ignored the packet outright, tested 3 times
per value with no reaction at all. This is categorically different from bytes 14/17 (which
still produced _some_ behavior, just not strobing) тАФ total silence is strong evidence this
byte is validated before the device will act on the packet at all.

**Cross-payload test confirms it: `0xAE` is required regardless of payload, not computed per
packet.** The same byte-18 sweep (`0x00`, `0x50`, `0xD0`, `0xFF`) was repeated on a
second, substantially different base packet тАФ different colors (4 distinct values instead of
one repeated color) and a different timing byte тАФ with byte 19 held fixed this time (an
earlier attempt at this test had accidentally varied byte 19 instead of byte 18). **All 4
non-`0xAE` values produced the identical result: complete non-response, confirmed watched and
observed, not just an unlogged batch.** If this byte were a real computed checksum, changing
the colors and timing byte should very plausibly have changed what value byte 18 needed to be
for a response тАФ it didn't. `0xAE` was required on both wildly different payloads. **This is
solid evidence byte 18 is a fixed constant this opcode expects, not a per-packet computed
value** тАФ likely a fixed protocol/format marker for E9 0E specifically, in the same spirit as
bytes 14 and 17, rather than a validation checksum in the traditional sense. The
"checksum-vs-vibration-byte" alternative theory is also resolved: a separate test confirmed
byte 19 can be changed freely with zero effect on timing, so byte 18 and byte 19 are
independent, and byte 18 is not simply a second vibration parameter.

**Practical takeaway: always use `0xAE` for byte 18 in this opcode**, regardless of what
colors or timing you're using тАФ no computation needed.

### Byte 19 - Vibration Pattern

See [EMCOT.world](https://emcot.world/Disney_MagicBand%2B_Bluetooth_Codes#Vibration_Palette) for more information.

**Confirmed independent of strobe timing.** A test varying this byte between `0xB0` and `0x00`
(holding everything else, including byte 18, fixed) produced identical ~25s on-time in both

interact with the strobe's visual timing.

## Building Your Own

```text
83 01 <E1|E2> 00 E9 0E 00 <TIMING> 0F <MASK+COLOR> <MASK+COLOR> <MASK+COLOR> <MASK+COLOR> <MASK+COLOR> 59 <BYTE15> <BYTE16> 48 AE <VIBRATION>
```

**Formula confirmed clean across the full tested range.** There is now a reliable formula for
picking a `<TIMING>` byte, with three flash-rate variants depending on mode:

```
Normal, scaler=0:             on_time = 1.5 ├Ч t    flashes = 3 ├Ч t         (2 flashes/sec)
Normal, scaler=1:             on_time = 3.0 ├Ч t    flashes = 1 ├Ч t         (1 flash/sec)
Extended-timeout, scaler=0:   on_time = 7.6 ├Ч t    flash rate ~0.3-0.4/sec (much slower, easier to count)
```

`byte = (extended << 7) | (scaler << 6) | (fade_bits << 4) | t`. **Fade timing does not follow
the E9 05/09 formula for this opcode тАФ confirmed, not just suspected.** `fade_bits=01`
consistently measures ~0.25s (confirmed across 4 total sends), about half the 0.5s the E9
05/09 formula would predict. `fade_bits=11` is stranger still тАФ rather than a simple longer
duration, it produces a **non-linear fade** that visibly slows down partway through, unlike
every other tested fade which was described as linear. Don't assume fade timing or shape
transfers from E9 05/09 for this opcode; test the specific `fade_bits` value you plan to use.

**`t=4` and `t=6`, previously flagged as exceptions, have been retested and now fit the
formula cleanly** тАФ the original odd readings were most likely one-off measurement errors, not
a real property of those values. The formula can now be trusted across the full 1-7 range.

**Worked example тАФ a ~10 second strobe:** `t тЙИ 10/1.5 тЙИ 7` (matches an actual tested value:
7 тЖТ 11.25s observed, 21 flashes) тЖТ `byte = (0<<7)|(0<<6)|(0b00<<4)|0b0111 = 0x07`.

**Worked example тАФ a slow strobe for manual flash-counting** (e.g. while testing something
else and needing to reliably count flashes by eye): use extended-timeout mode at a small `t`,
e.g. `t=2` тЖТ `byte = (1<<7)|(0<<6)|(0b00<<4)|0b0010 = 0x82`, confirmed at 15s on, 4-6 flashes,
~1s fade тАФ roughly one flash every 2.5-3.75 seconds, much easier to count than the base 2/sec
rate.

### `<MASK+COLOR>` (byte per LED zone)

Same model as E9 09: `mask_color = (mask << 5) | color_index`, mask=`101` in every packet
seen so far. Zone order confirmed as Center, Top-Right, Bottom-Right, Bottom-Left, Top-Left.

## Suggested follow-up tests

1. **Characterize the `fade_bits=11` non-linear fade curve more precisely.** It's been
   described qualitatively as slowing down partway through, but not measured тАФ noting
   approximate brightness at a few points through the fade (e.g. "still bright at 1s in,
   nearly out by 2s") would help characterize the actual curve shape, not just confirm it's
   non-linear.
2. **The E100/E200 "restart vs. continue" hypothesis is deliberately deferred, not
   abandoned.** It's ruled out as the explanation for the `t=4`/`t=6` anomaly specifically
   (both used the same prefix). Rather than test it in isolation on this one opcode, the plan
   is to hold off until more opcodes (E9 06, 08, 0C, 11, 12, etc.) have been characterized тАФ
   if bytes 2-3 have a real, consistent meaning, it's most likely opcode-agnostic, and testing
   it once across several already-understood opcodes at once will be both more conclusive and
   less wasted effort than repeating a single-opcode test for each one individually. See the
   cross-opcode note at the top of this doc's Bytes 2-3 section.
